Landing Page Conversion Rate Benchmarks by Industry (2026)

By Racoonn Team · 2026-03-27 · 6 min read

The Benchmark You're Probably Comparing Yourself To Is Wrong

The often-cited '2.35% average landing page conversion rate' comes from a Wordstream study that's over a decade old, based primarily on paid search landing pages. Using it as a universal benchmark is like using the average car speed to evaluate a race car. Context matters enormously.

Conversion rate varies by traffic source, offer type, price point, industry, and what counts as a 'conversion'. A SaaS free trial signup is different from an e-commerce purchase, which is different from an email list opt-in. Comparing across these categories produces misleading conclusions.

Benchmarks by Traffic Source

Email traffic: 5–10% conversion rate typical. Email subscribers are your most engaged audience — they already know and trust you. Paid search: 2–5% for commercial keywords, 1–3% for informational keywords. Social media: 0.5–2%, with significant variance by platform and audience. Direct/branded: 4–8%, since visitors typing your URL have high intent. Organic search: 1–4%, varies greatly by keyword intent.

Interpret your conversion rate in the context of where your traffic comes from. A 1% conversion rate from cold social traffic is excellent. A 1% conversion rate from email subscribers is a signal to investigate.

Benchmarks by Offer Type

Free trial (no credit card): 3–8%. Waitlist signup: 5–15% when product urgency is high. Email opt-in with lead magnet: 15–30% when highly relevant. Paid purchase under $50: 1–3%. Paid purchase $50–200: 0.5–2%. Demo request: 2–5%.

Lower price points and lower commitment actions have higher natural conversion rates. A page asking for an email address will always convert at a higher rate than a page asking for a credit card. Benchmark within your offer type.

What Top Performers Do Differently

Wordstream's analysis of the top 10% of landing pages found they convert at 3–5× the average. The pattern: extremely specific value propositions (not generic), social proof tied to specific outcomes (not generic testimonials), minimal form fields, and fast page load times.

The top-converting pages treat every element as functional: every word earns its place, every visual serves a purpose, every friction source has been eliminated. This isn't aesthetic minimalism — it's conversion engineering.

How to Set Your Own Benchmarks

The most useful benchmark is your own historical performance. If your landing page converted at 2% last quarter and now converts at 3%, you've improved by 50% regardless of what the industry average is. Track your conversion rate over time and measure improvements against your own baseline.

Segment your conversion rate by traffic source, device type, and new vs returning visitors. Each segment has different natural conversion rates and different optimization levers. A single aggregate rate hides the information you need to improve.
