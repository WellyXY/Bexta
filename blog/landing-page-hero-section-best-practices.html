How to Write a Landing Page Hero That Converts

By Racoonn Team · 2026-03-27 · 6 min read

The 5-Second Rule for Hero Sections

Research by the Nielsen Norman Group shows that users form opinions about websites within 50 milliseconds of viewing them. In practical terms, your hero section must communicate — within 5 seconds of arrival — what the product does, who it's for, and why they should care. If it fails this test, visitors leave before reading anything else.

The five-second test is one of the simplest and most valuable user research methods: show someone your landing page for exactly 5 seconds, then cover it and ask three questions: What does this product do? Who is it for? What would you do next? If they can't answer the first two questions correctly, your hero is failing.

The Anatomy of a High-Converting Hero

Headline: the single most important copy element. Should state the core value in 10–15 words. The best headlines are specific: 'Find out why users leave your landing page in 28 minutes' beats 'The future of user research'.

Sub-headline (1–2 sentences): expands on the headline with one more detail — the mechanism or the target audience. 'Racoonn simulates 5,000 AI personas on your URL and delivers a prioritized fix list before your next standup.'

Hero visual: shows the product in action, or visualizes the outcome the customer gets. Not stock photos of people at computers. A real screenshot, dashboard, or output from the product.

Primary CTA: one action, specific label. 'Start free trial' or 'Test my landing page free' beats 'Get started'.

Headline Formulas That Work

[Outcome] without [pain]: 'Get user research insights without recruiting real users.' [Time frame] + [specific outcome]: 'See why your landing page is losing conversions in 28 minutes.' [Who it's for] + [specific benefit]: 'For SaaS founders who need to know why users aren't converting.'

The formula to avoid: '[Vague superlative] [category descriptor] for [generic audience]' — 'The most powerful analytics platform for modern teams'. This describes nothing and promises nothing.

The Role of the Hero Visual

The hero visual should answer 'what does this look like?' — not 'what does this feel like?'. Product screenshots with real data convert better than lifestyle illustrations. Dashboard views that show the output a user will get convert better than abstract animations.

If your product has a compelling visual output — a report, a chart, a code snippet, a before-and-after — show it in the hero. Visitors want to see what they're signing up for. Give them a preview of the value.

Mobile Hero Optimization

On mobile, your hero has less vertical space and text renders smaller. The headline should be 8–12 words maximum for mobile, the sub-headline should be 1 sentence, and the CTA should be visible without scrolling at 375px width.

Test your hero on the most common mobile device sizes: 375px (iPhone SE/most iPhones), 390px (iPhone 14), and 412px (large Android). If any of these hide your CTA below the fold, your mobile hero is losing conversions.

Frequently Asked Questions

8–15 words is the optimal range. Long enough to communicate specific value, short enough to be read in one glance. Under 8 words is often too vague; over 20 words loses attention.

Both can work. Questions create curiosity ('Why are users leaving your landing page?'). Statements make claims ('Find out exactly why users leave your landing page'). Test both with your specific audience. Questions tend to work better for awareness-stage traffic; statements for high-intent traffic.

Real product screenshots with real data, showing the output the user gets. Avoid stock photos, abstract illustrations, or animations that don't communicate product value. The question to ask: does this visual help a visitor understand what they'll get?

For low-price or free products, including pricing in the hero reduces hesitation and improves qualified conversions. For enterprise products or complex pricing, the hero should focus on value and defer pricing to a dedicated section.
